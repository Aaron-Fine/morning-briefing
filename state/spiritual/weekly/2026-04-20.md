# Come, Follow Me — April 20–26, 2026
## "All That the Lord Hath Spoken We Will Do"
### Exodus 19–20; 24; 31–34

---

## 1. Lesson Overview

The Sinai narrative is the theological heart of the Torah. Everything before it — the patriarchs, Egypt, the Exodus — leads up to it. Everything after it — the wilderness, the cultic codes, the conquest — flows out of it. What happens at Sinai is that a liberated but disorganized group of former slaves becomes a *covenant people*.

The themes that dominate this week:

- **Theophany** — God's direct, communal self-revelation.
- **Covenant** — the binding of Israel to YHWH with stipulations, ratification, and signs.
- **Mediation** — Moses standing between God and the people, and the tension between direct and mediated encounter.
- **Apostasy** — breaking covenant almost immediately after making it.
- **Divine mercy and its limits** — the second tablets, the "Thirteen Attributes," and what God withdraws as well as what He gives.
- **Transformation** — Moses's shining face; what happens to someone who has stood in God's presence.

From a Latter-day Saint angle, this is where covenant as a two-way binding relationship gets its clearest Old Testament expression, and where the JST makes some of its most consequential textual changes in the entire Bible. The LDS reading of these chapters is genuinely different from how Jewish, Catholic, and Protestant traditions read them — worth knowing when it's different and why.

## 2. Narrative Unit Summaries

### Unit 1 — Exodus 19: Arrival at Sinai

Israel reaches Sinai three months after Egypt. God offers them a covenant: *"if ye will obey my voice indeed, and keep my covenant, then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people … and ye shall be unto me a kingdom of priests, and an holy nation"* (19:5–6). The people agree before they know the terms: "All that the Lord hath spoken we will do" — the phrase that names this week's lesson. Then comes the theophany: smoke, fire, trumpet, a shaking mountain. The people are terrified and ask Moses to mediate. The pattern is established: Israel approaches God through Moses.

**LDS interpretive key:** D&C 84:19–25 is the lens to read this whole week through. Moses is trying to sanctify Israel so they can *see God's face* and receive the Melchizedek Priesthood. They refuse the invitation. The theophany that terrifies them was supposed to be an initiation, not just an ordeal.

### Unit 2 — Exodus 20: The Decalogue

The "Ten Words" (Hebrew *aseret ha-devarim*, not quite "commandments") are spoken directly by God to the whole assembly. The structure divides into obligations toward God (1–4) and toward neighbor (5–10). Crucially, the Decalogue begins not with a command but with a *historical reminder*: "I am the Lord thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt" (20:2). **Grace precedes law.** Israel's obligations flow from what God has already done, not the reverse. At the end, the people again ask Moses to mediate — "let not God speak with us, lest we die" (20:19).

**JST note:** Minor tightening in ch. 20; no major narrative change. The most important LDS lens here comes not from the JST but from D&C 84:23–25, which reads Israel's retreat from direct encounter as a missed opportunity for a higher order.

### Unit 3 — Exodus 24: Covenant Ratification

Moses builds an altar with twelve pillars for the tribes, performs burnt and peace offerings, and conducts a blood ritual: half the blood is thrown on the altar, half on the people, with the words "Behold the blood of the covenant" (24:8). Then *seventy-four men* — Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and seventy elders — ascend the mountain, and "they saw the God of Israel … they did eat and drink" (24:10–11). A communal covenant meal in God's presence. This is one of the most astonishing passages in the Torah and one that most Christian devotional literature quietly avoids, because it undercuts easy claims that the Old Testament God is distant and unseeable.

**JST note:** JST adjustments emphasize Moses's unique mediator role, consistent with the D&C 84 framing of a higher priesthood that Moses holds and the rest do not fully share.

### Unit 4 — Exodus 31: Sabbath as Covenant Sign

After the tabernacle instructions (chs. 25–31, mostly outside our reading), ch. 31 closes with the Sabbath as "a perpetual covenant … a sign between me and the children of Israel for ever" (31:16–17). The Sabbath isn't ancillary; it's the defining *mark* of the covenant people. The chapter ends with God handing Moses "two tables of testimony, tables of stone, written with the finger of God" (31:18).

### Unit 5 — Exodus 32: The Golden Calf and Intercession

Moses is on the mountain too long. Israel asks Aaron to make them "gods, which shall go before us" (32:1). Aaron fashions a calf; the people declare, "These be thy gods, O Israel, which brought thee up out of the land of Egypt." Aaron then builds an altar and proclaims *"a feast to the Lord [YHWH]"* the next day (32:5) — strongly suggesting the calf was intended as a *pedestal or throne for YHWH*, not a rival deity. The sin is therefore subtler and more damning: making the covenant God tangible, manageable, useful.

God threatens destruction. Moses intercedes, arguing from God's reputation and the promises to the patriarchs (32:11–13). He descends, breaks the tablets, grinds the calf, and the Levites execute the ringleaders.

**JST note (substantive):** The KJV at 32:14 says "the Lord repented of the evil which he thought to do unto his people," which is theologically awkward — does God change His mind? The JST reframes this: the mercy is contingent on human repentance rather than on God having a change of heart. This relocates the dynamic from "God vacillates" to "God responds to repentance," which fits better with the LDS commitment to a God who "changeth not" in His purposes.

### Unit 6 — Exodus 33: Moses Seeks to See God

After the calf, God says He will send Israel onward but not go in their midst — His presence would consume a "stiffnecked people" (33:3). Moses pleads. Then Moses asks to see God's glory. God hides Moses in the cleft of the rock and passes by; Moses sees God's "back parts" but not His face (33:23). Earlier in the same chapter, though, the narrator has already said Moses spoke with God "face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend" (33:11).

**JST note (substantive):** KJV 33:20 — "there shall no man see me, and live" — is qualified in the JST. The JST adds language to the effect that no *sinful* person can see God and live; when properly sanctified and quickened by the Spirit, a person can. This harmonizes with Moses 1 (where Moses clearly sees God face to face, sustained by God's glory) and with the plain report of 33:11 that Moses and God spoke as friends. The LDS reading is that theophany is possible for the prepared, not categorically impossible.

### Unit 7 — Exodus 34: The Second Tablets

Moses *hews* new tablets himself — the first set had been made by God — and returns up the mountain. God proclaims His name in what is arguably the most important self-disclosure in the Hebrew Bible: "The Lord, The Lord God, merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth, keeping mercy for thousands, forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty" (34:6–7). In Jewish tradition these are the **Thirteen Attributes of Mercy**, recited on Yom Kippur. Moses returns with his face shining so intensely that he has to veil himself before the people.

**JST note (most substantive JST change in this week's reading):** KJV 34:1–2 says the new tablets will bear "the words that were in the first tables, which thou brakest." The JST changes this: the second tablets do *not* contain the same content. The higher law and the Melchizedek Priesthood that were on the first tablets are withdrawn; the second tablets contain only the law of carnal commandments (the Aaronic/lesser law). D&C 84:23–27 is the cross-reference that ties this together. **This is a distinctively LDS reading.** No other major tradition — Jewish, Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant — reads the second tablets as a downgrade. It's worth knowing when we are departing from the mainstream and why.

## 3. Key Scriptures

**Exodus 19:5–6** — *"ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto me above all people … a kingdom of priests, and an holy nation."*
"Peculiar treasure" (Hebrew *segulah*) means "personal possession" — like a king's private treasury, kept apart from the royal stores. Not *odd*, but *belonging to*. "Kingdom of priests" (*mamlekhet kohanim*) is a collective priestly vocation: the whole nation is to mediate God to the world. 1 Peter 2:9 picks up this language for the church. D&C 84:33–34 expands it into the oath and covenant of the priesthood — those who receive both priesthoods and magnify their calling become "the sons of Moses and of Aaron and the seed of Abraham."

**Exodus 20:2–3** — *"I am the Lord thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt … Thou shalt have no other gods before me."*
The command is grounded in redemption already accomplished. Grace precedes law. "Before me" (*al-panai*) literally means "in my face" or "in my presence" — which can be read as "in preference to me" or "in addition to me." Either way, this is not philosophical monotheism; it is covenant exclusivity. You don't worship the one who rescued you *and* worship rival powers.

**Exodus 24:9–11** — *"they saw the God of Israel … and upon the nobles of the children of Israel he laid not his hand: also they saw God, and did eat and drink."*
Seventy-four people see God and share a meal in His presence. The Hebrew *ra'ah* is emphatic: they *saw*. The narrator sounds almost startled that God did not strike them down. This is the Old Testament's most direct analog to sacramental fellowship and the single strongest internal counter-reading to the bare "no one can see God and live."

**Exodus 32:32** — *"Yet now, if thou wilt forgive their sin—; and if not, blot me, I pray thee, out of thy book which thou hast written."*
Moses offers himself in exchange. The Hebrew breaks off mid-sentence — "if thou wilt forgive their sin—" — as though he cannot finish the thought. One of the great intercessory moments of scripture, foreshadowing Christ and modeling a mediator who *argues* with God on behalf of a failing covenant people rather than passively transmitting divine decrees.

**Exodus 34:6–7** — *"The Lord, The Lord God, merciful and gracious … forgiving iniquity and transgression and sin, and that will by no means clear the guilty."*
God's self-disclosure of His own name. The tension is crucial: God is *both* "merciful and gracious" *and* "will by no means clear the guilty." Modern audiences keep wanting one or the other — cheap grace or harsh justice. The covenant God is both. And this self-disclosure comes *right after* the golden calf — which means God reveals Himself as supremely merciful in the immediate aftermath of the worst covenant breach yet.

## 4. Language and Cultural Context

**Covenant as political-legal form.** The structure of Exodus 19–24 mirrors ancient Near Eastern suzerain-vassal treaties — a form well attested from Hittite and Assyrian archives in the second millennium BCE. These treaties follow a predictable shape: preamble naming the great king, historical prologue recalling what the king has done for the vassal, stipulations, provisions for deposit and public reading, witnesses, blessings and curses. When you read Exodus 20 against this template, the "historical prologue" ("I am the Lord thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of Egypt") is not throwaway preamble — it is the *legal basis* on which every following obligation rests. George Mendenhall's mid-20th-century work established this parallel, and it has held up well. What's distinctive at Sinai is that the vassal is a whole people rather than a king, and the content includes moral and cultic material beyond mere political loyalty.

**"Peculiar" doesn't mean weird.** KJV "peculiar treasure" (*segulah*) means "personal, private possession." Think wedding ring, not oddity.

**"Thou shalt not kill" is a translation problem.** The Hebrew is *lo tirtzach*. *Ratzach* refers to *unlawful* killing — roughly "murder" — not all killing. The KJV's "kill" is broader than the Hebrew warrants, and the distinction matters because this verse gets deployed across debates about war, capital punishment, self-defense, and abortion in ways the actual verb does not straightforwardly support on *either* side. Robert Alter and most scholarly translations render it "You shall not murder."

**The golden calf probably wasn't what you think.** ANE iconography shows bull and calf imagery as common pedestals on which a deity was imagined to stand. Aaron declares "a feast to YHWH" the day after making the calf (32:5). The calf was very likely intended not as a rival god but as a physical *throne or locus* for the covenant God. The sin is therefore subtler and more disturbing than worshiping a foreign deity: it is collapsing the invisible covenant God into an object the community can see, touch, and manage. This is more unsettling, not less — because it means idolatry can look exactly like worshipping the *right* God the *wrong* way.

**Sabbath as economic claim.** Exodus 20:10 explicitly includes servants, strangers, and livestock in the rest command. In an agricultural slave society, requiring that enslaved laborers and foreigners also rest is a radical economic obligation. Rest is not a luxury good for owners; it is a covenant right for everyone in the household, including those with no legal standing. Walter Brueggemann has written at length on Sabbath as resistance to the logic of endless production that Israel had just come out of in Egypt.

**Covenant meal.** "They saw God, and did eat and drink" (24:11). In ANE culture, shared meals *sealed* covenants — to eat with someone was to enter solemn relationship with them. The sacramental resonance for Latter-day Saints is hard to miss.

**"Back parts" (33:23).** Hebrew *achor* means "behind" or "after." Some scholars render it "aftereffects" — what remains after God has passed. The point in the KJV reading is that full direct glory is too much but something can be glimpsed. The JST takes this in a different direction entirely, reframing the limit as one of human preparedness rather than categorical impossibility.

## 5. Modern-Day Insights

A note on sourcing: I'm being deliberately cautious here, because it's easy to attribute specific wording to scholars when all you actually remember is the substance of their argument. Where I'm paraphrasing rather than quoting, I'll say so.

**Hugh Nibley on Sinai and covenant form.** Nibley wrote extensively on the ANE covenant structure of Exodus and on its parallels with ancient temple liturgies. His general argument, spread across pieces in the Collected Works and *An Approach to the Book of Mormon*, was that the covenant pattern at Sinai — ritual meal, ratification, deposit of the covenant document in a sacred place — preserves an ancient pattern that still shows up in LDS temple worship. I'm paraphrasing the general thrust rather than quoting specific lines, because I don't have confidence in reproducing his exact wording.

**John Walton on the tabernacle as covenant extension.** Walton, in *The Lost World of the Israelite Conquest* and related books, argues that the Sinai pericope as a whole is the establishment of sacred space — God taking up residence among His people. The tabernacle instructions that immediately follow chs. 25–31 are not a digression from the covenant; they are its natural extension. The covenant requires a dwelling place for God among His people. For Latter-day Saints, this framing ties Sinai directly to temple theology in a way that's hard to see on a first reading.

**D&C 84:19–27 as the LDS interpretive key.** Not an outside source, but worth flagging as the single most important lens through which Latter-day Saints read *all* of this week's material. The doctrine there is that Moses sought to sanctify Israel so they could "behold the face of God" (v. 23), but they "hardened their hearts" (v. 24), and "the lesser priesthood continued" (v. 26) while the higher was withdrawn. No other Christian or Jewish tradition reads the text this way. Worth knowing when we are reading with the tradition and when we are reading against it.

**Robert Alter on the literary structure of the calf narrative.** Alter has pointed out (paraphrasing his general argument in *The Art of Biblical Narrative* and his Five Books commentary) that the golden calf episode is deliberately structured in contrast to the tabernacle instructions. Both involve freewill offerings of gold. Both involve craftsmen. Both produce a visible center for worship. The difference is that the tabernacle is *commanded* and the calf is a *human initiative to make God manageable*. Alter's broader point — that biblical narrative does theological work through literary structure — is a useful posture for this whole reading.

**Archaeological context.** We have no direct evidence of the Sinai theophany itself, which is unsurprising. But we do have excellent ANE evidence for the treaty forms the covenant follows, the calf iconography the golden calf episode presupposes, and portable tent-shrines comparable to the tabernacle in other ANE cultures. This corroborates that the text is grounded in a real second-millennium context, which is helpful though not dispositive for historicity debates.

## 6. Current Event Mapping — Use and Misuse

### Part A: Misuse in Current Discourse

**1. The Ten Commandments as civic monument.**

Since Louisiana's 2024 law requiring the Decalogue in public classrooms, several other states have passed or proposed similar measures. The framing is typically "these are the foundation of American law and morality."

What's wrong with this use:

First, the Decalogue is explicitly framed in ch. 20 as *covenant stipulations* for a people God has already redeemed. Strip it of that frame and you are left with generic moral rules plus specifically religious obligations — no other gods, no idols, Sabbath observance — that are incoherent outside a covenant with YHWH. Posting "Thou shalt have no other gods before me" in a pluralistic classroom turns a covenant loyalty claim into either empty civic decoration or sectarian imposition. The text supports neither.

Second, "the" Ten Commandments is already a selection. Jewish, Catholic, Lutheran, and Reformed traditions all number and divide them differently. The version usually posted is the Protestant numbering.

Third — and this is where it gets uncomfortable for members of a covenant-making people — this kind of civic deployment *cheapens* the commandments. It trains Christians (including Latter-day Saints who go along with it) to treat the Decalogue as a tribal identity marker rather than as a demanding covenant obligation. A cheap symbolic win that costs nothing. Meanwhile, religious minorities — Jews, Muslims, Hindus, atheists, and yes, Catholics and Latter-day Saints whose tradition reads the text differently — are signaled that they are guests in a Protestant Christian civic space.

A Latter-day Saint should be *especially* alert to this misuse. We of all people understand covenants as serious binding relationships rather than cultural signaling. The move to treat the Decalogue as civic furniture is, by our own theology, a category error.

**2. "Golden calf" as all-purpose political insult.**

Every faction now calls the other side's beloved thing a golden calf. Progressives say MAGA is a golden calf. Conservatives say "woke" ideology is a golden calf. Critics of both say the other party has built one. The move is cheap because it is frictionless — label what you dislike as idolatry and you are done.

What is missed: the calf was almost certainly meant as a pedestal for YHWH, not a rival god. Aaron calls it a feast to YHWH. The actual sin is making the covenant God *tangible, manageable, useful*. That pattern is much harder to spot because it looks pious from the inside. The version of God who conveniently endorses your politics, blesses your economic arrangements, and hates who you hate — *that* is the calf. Every faction, including religious ones, is capable of this, and usually is doing it.

Who pays: the people being called idolaters (usually dehumanized by the charge), but also — importantly — the person making the charge, who gets to avoid the uncomfortable question of whether their own tangible, useful God is itself a calf.

What the text actually says: Aaron was meeting a *legitimate* need. "Up, make us gods to go before us, for as for this Moses … we wot not what is become of him" (32:1). The people were frightened and abandoned. The calf was a pastoral failure as much as a theological one. A faithful reading asks where *you* are currently meeting legitimate spiritual hunger with something tangible but false — not where the other tribe is.

**3. "Thou shalt not kill" weaponized in political debates.**

The KJV rendering gets used to shut down discussion of war, capital punishment, self-defense, and abortion — usually by whichever side finds it rhetorically convenient in the moment. The Hebrew *ratzach* refers specifically to unlawful killing, which is why the Torah itself prescribes capital punishment and commands warfare without apparent self-contradiction. That does not mean the commandment is silent on war or capital punishment — Scripture has a great deal to say about those — but "thou shalt not kill, full stop" is not what the sixth commandment says.

Who pays: people on all sides of genuinely hard ethical questions about state violence get a flattened, mistranslated slogan instead of careful moral reasoning from scripture. The people who most *need* that reasoning — people in actual moral dilemmas — get bumper stickers.

What the text does: the Decalogue asserts that human life has covenant dignity. The specific content of that protection has to be worked out with the rest of scripture. A thoughtful Latter-day Saint should be suspicious of anyone — left or right — who deploys "thou shalt not kill" as a conversation-ender.

**Conspicuously absent from popular discourse:**

- **Moses's bold intercession.** Our contemporary devotional culture rarely celebrates that Moses *argued God down* from destroying Israel. "Your reputation is on the line, remember your promises, I want no part of a do-over if it abandons these people." This is a model of prophet-as-advocate-for-the-people — arguing upward to God on behalf of the failing community — that our current religious culture, which tends to treat directives as things to be received and transmitted downward uncritically, does not preach much.

- **Sabbath as economic justice.** Exodus 20:10's inclusion of servants, strangers, and livestock is a radical economic claim: rest is a right for the people in the household with no legal power. Most popular Sabbath preaching is about personal piety or worker self-care. The original text is a *protection for the people with no standing* — politically inconvenient enough that conservatives (who don't want labor obligations) and progressives (who often treat Sabbath observance as cultural conservatism) both tend to skip it.

- **The sheer strangeness of Exodus 24:11.** Seventy-four people *saw God and ate with Him.* This ought to destabilize any reading of God as distant, abstract, or categorically unseeable. LDS theology takes it seriously. Most popular Christian discourse quietly walks past it.

### Part B: Faithful Application

**1. Covenant over civic religion.**

The Sinai covenant is specific, demanding, and transformative. A Latter-day Saint in 2026 might ask: am I treating my temple covenants with the seriousness that Israel was asked to treat theirs, or as cultural markers and community belonging? The posture "all that the Lord hath spoken we will do" (19:8) is meant to produce lives shaped by costly obligation, not tribal identification. If our covenants are producing the latter instead of the former, we are doing to them what the culture does to the Decalogue — turning binding obligation into symbol.

**2. Mediate like Moses, not like a PR rep.**

Moses's intercession in ch. 32 is a model for leadership in any covenant community. Moses does not manage downward from God to the people; he *advocates upward* from the people to God. He argues, pleads, offers himself as substitute. For anyone in a ministering, leadership, or parenting role — including the daily work of holding relationships together in a divided political and religious environment — the Mosaic model is costly advocacy *on behalf of the people*, not smooth transmission of directives. What would it look like to argue God-ward on behalf of the people who have most disappointed you?

**3. Read the whole story, including the second tablets.**

The JST and D&C 84 frame the second tablets as a concession: Israel lost something real in the golden calf episode, and the law they received after was *less* than what they could have had. This is sobering. It implies that covenant breakage has real costs, that some opportunities don't simply repeat identically, and that God's mercy often takes the form of "I will still meet you, but at a different register."

For a person who has broken faith with God in any register — and who hasn't — this reading offers both real comfort *and* real honesty. God still gives you tablets. They may not be the same tablets. And the shining face of Moses in 34:29 comes after the *second* set, not the first. Transformation is still available after failure — which may be the most pastorally important thing in this week's reading.

### Questions for Study

- Where in my life am I making something tangible to manage anxiety about a God who feels far away? What would it look like to tolerate the absence and keep the covenant instead?
- If the Sabbath is protection for the people in my household who have the least power — hired workers, or my employees, or my kids — how does my Sabbath observance actually serve that purpose?
- Moses argued with God over a people who had just betrayed him. Who am I being asked to argue God-ward on behalf of right now, even when they have disappointed me?
- What might God be showing me in "back parts" — the aftereffects of His presence — that I keep wanting to see head-on and in full?
